CHAPTER 1: PHYSICAL QUANTITIES AND MEASUREMENT

PAGE 1: INTRODUCTION

1.1 What is Physics?

Physics is the fundamental branch of science that deals with the study of matter, its composition, properties, and interactions with energy. It seeks to understand the natural phenomena occurring in the universe through observation, experimentation, and mathematical analysis.

1.2 Branches of Physics

The branches of Physics are classified on the basis of different areas of study with different approaches. These include mechanics, thermodynamics, optics, electromagnetism, atomic physics, nuclear physics, and quantum physics, among others.

1.3 Types of Physicists

  • Theoretical Physicists: Develop mathematical models and theories to explain physical phenomena.
  • Experimental Physicists: Conduct experiments and observations to test theories and discover new phenomena.

1.4 Physical Quantities

Physics defines mathematical relationships between physical quantities. A physical quantity is any property that can be measured and expressed numerically. Every physical quantity has two essential components:

  1. Magnitude (numerical value)
  2. Unit (standard of measurement)

1.5 Classification of Physical Quantities

(i) Base or Fundamental Quantities: These are independent quantities that cannot be defined in terms of other quantities. The seven base quantities are:

(ii) Derived Physical Quantities: These are quantities that are derived from base quantities through mathematical operations. Examples include area, volume, speed, velocity, acceleration, force, energy, power, etc.

PAGE 2: MEASUREMENT STANDARDS

2.1 Standards of Measurement

Length: The standard unit of length is the meter (m). Measured using: Measuring tape (for longer distances) or Meter rule (for shorter distances).

Mass: The standard unit of mass is the kilogram (kg). Measured using a physical balance or electronic balance.

Time: The standard unit of time is the second (s). Measured using: Stopwatch (for short intervals) or Clock (for longer durations).

2.2 Scientific Notation

Scientific notation is a way of writing numbers in the form: a × 10ⁿ where 'a' is a number between 1 and 10 and 'n' is an integer (positive or negative).

Examples: Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s; Diameter of atom = 1 × 10⁻¹⁰ m.

2.3 Measurement of Volume

Volume of Liquids: Calculated or measured with the help of a measuring cylinder marked in milliliters (mL) or cubic centimeters (cm³).

Volume of Irregular Objects: Calculated using the water displacement method:

Volume of object = V₂ - V₁

Prefixes are added to the base units to indicate multiples or submultiples.

PAGE 3: DENSITY & SIGNIFICANT FIGURES

3.1 Density

Density is defined as the ratio of mass per unit volume.

Density (ρ) = Mass (m) / Volume (V)

Units: SI unit: kg/m³; CGS unit: g/cm³. Density of water = 1000 kg/m³ or 1 g/cm³.

3.2 Specific Gravity (Relative Density)

Specific Gravity = Density of Substance / Density of Water

Dimensionless quantity. If specific gravity > 1, the substance sinks in water. If < 1, it floats.

3.3 Significant Figures

The most accurate or reliable numbers of a value representing the precision of a measurement.

Rules:
  • All non-zero digits are significant.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant.
  • Trailing zeros after decimal point ARE significant.
  • Trailing zeros in whole numbers without decimal may or may not be significant.

Applications in Daily Life:
Construction and engineering, Medicine and healthcare, Cooking and baking, Sports and athletics, Weather forecasting.

CHAPTER 2: KINEMATICS - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

PAGE 1: FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS OF MOTION

2.1 Rest and Motion

Rest: A body is said to be at rest if it does not change its position with respect to its surroundings.

Motion: A body is said to be in motion if it changes its position with respect to its surroundings.

Note: Rest and motion are relative terms. A body may be at rest with respect to one object and in motion with respect to another.

2.2 Types of Motion

1. Translatory Motion: When all points of a moving body move uniformly along the same straight line.

Types of Translatory Motion:

  • Linear Motion: Motion along a straight line. (Example: Car moving on a straight road)
  • Circular Motion: Motion along a circular path. (Example: Earth revolving around the Sun)
  • Random/Irregular Motion: Irregular, zigzag motion. (Example: Motion of gas molecules)

2. Rotatory/Spin Motion: The motion of a body around a fixed axis which passes through the body itself.

  • Example: Spinning top, rotation of Earth about its axis.

3. Vibratory/Oscillatory Motion: The back and forth motion of a body about its mean position.

  • Example: Motion of pendulum, vibration of guitar string.
PAGE 2: SCALAR AND VECTOR QUANTITIES

2.3 Distance and Displacement

Distance: The total length covered by a moving body without mentioning direction. It is a scalar quantity. SI Unit: meter (m).

Displacement: The distance measured in a straight line in a particular direction. It is a vector quantity. SI Unit: meter (m).

Key Difference:
  • Distance ≥ Displacement
  • Distance is path-dependent; displacement is path-independent.

2.4 Speed and Velocity

Speed: Distance covered by an object in unit time. Scalar quantity.

Speed (v) = Distance (d) / Time (t)

Types of Speed: Uniform Speed, Variable Speed, Average Speed.

Velocity: Rate of change of displacement with respect to time. Vector quantity.

Velocity (v) = Displacement (s) / Time (t)

Types of Velocity: Uniform Velocity, Variable Velocity, Average Velocity.

2.5 Acceleration

Definition: Rate of change of velocity of an object with respect to time. Vector quantity.

a = (v - u) / t

where: a = acceleration, v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, t = time. SI Unit: m/s².

Types: Positive Acceleration (velocity increases), Negative Acceleration/Retardation (velocity decreases).

PAGE 3: EQUATIONS OF MOTION

2.6 Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion

First Equation: v = u + at

Second Equation: s = ut + ½at²

Third Equation: v² = u² + 2as

2.7 Special Case: Equations for Free Fall

When a body falls freely under gravity (acceleration a = g, height s = h):

  1. v = u + gt
  2. h = ut + ½gt²
  3. v² = u² + 2gh
Value of g ≈ 9.8 m/s² (often taken as 10 m/s² for calculations).

CHAPTER 3: DYNAMICS - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

PAGE 1: FOUNDATIONS OF DYNAMICS, FORCE, MOMENTUM & INERTIA

3.1 What is Dynamics?

Dynamics is the branch of mechanics that studies the causes of motion. While kinematics describes how objects move, dynamics explains why they move by analyzing forces, mass, and interactions.

3.2 Force: The Agent of Change

Definition: A push or pull that can change the state of rest or uniform motion of a body, or alter its shape/direction.

  • Nature: Vector quantity.
  • SI Unit: Newton (N) | 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².

3.3 Momentum: Quantity of Motion

Definition: Momentum is the product of mass and velocity. It quantifies how difficult it is to stop a moving object.

p = m × v

Vector quantity; direction same as velocity. Conservation Principle: In an isolated system, total momentum remains constant.

3.4 Newton’s First Law of Motion & Inertia

Statement: "A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force."

Inertia: The inherent property of matter to resist changes in its state of rest or motion. It is directly proportional to mass.

TypeDefinition
Inertia of RestTendency to remain at rest. (Example: Passengers fall backward when bus starts)
Inertia of MotionTendency to continue moving. (Example: Passengers lean forward when brakes applied)
Inertia of DirectionTendency to maintain straight-line path.
PAGE 2: NEWTON’S LAWS & CIRCULAR MOTION

3.5 Newton’s Second Law of Motion

The acceleration of a body is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

F = m × a

Force is also the rate of change of momentum: F = Δp / Δt

3.6 Newton’s Third Law of Motion

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Action and reaction forces act on different bodies and therefore do not cancel each other.

3.7 Uniform Circular Motion

Motion in a circular path at constant speed. Requires a continuous inward force.

Centripetal ForceKeeps body in circular pathInwardF_c = mv²/r
Centrifugal ForceApparent outward tendencyOutward-
PAGE 3: FRICTION & SAFETY

3.8 Friction: The Resistive Force

The force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces in contact.

  • Static Friction: Acts when body is at rest.
  • Limiting Friction: Max static friction (F_s = μ_sN).
  • Kinetic Friction: Acts during sliding (F_k = μ_kN).
Rolling Friction is much smaller than sliding friction.

3.9 Safety Devices

Based on Impulse-Momentum Theorem (FΔt = Δp). To reduce injury (force), we must increase the time of impact.

  • Seatbelts/Airbags: Increase stopping time to decrease force.
  • Crumple Zones: Absorb impact energy by increasing collision duration.

CHAPTER 4: TURNING EFFECT OF FORCES - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

PAGE 1: PARALLEL FORCES & RESOLUTION OF FORCES

4.1 Parallel Forces

Forces whose lines of action are parallel to each other are called parallel forces.

TypeDefinitionExample
Like Parallel ForcesParallel forces acting in the same directionTwo people pushing a box in the same direction
Unlike Parallel ForcesParallel forces acting in opposite directionsTug of war, forces on opposite sides

4.2 Resultant Force

The sum of two or more forces acting on a body is called the resultant of forces.

Head to Tail Rule: The graphical method for addition of forces. Place the tail of the second vector at the head of the first vector. The resultant is drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector.

4.3 Resolution of Forces

Splitting of a force into two perpendicular components is called resolution of force.

Rectangular Components:

Fx = F cosθ
Fy = F sinθ

Finding Magnitude and Direction:

F = √(Fx² + Fy²)
θ = tan⁻¹(Fy/Fx)
PAGE 2: MOMENT OF FORCE (TORQUE)

4.4 Turning Effect of Force (Torque)

The turning effect of a force about an axis or pivot is called the moment of force or torque.

τ = F × L

Where: τ = Torque (N·m), F = Force (N), L = Moment arm (m).

  • Torque is a vector quantity.
  • SI Unit: Newton-meter (N·m).
  • Clockwise moment: Negative (-)
  • Anticlockwise moment: Positive (+)
PAGE 3: PRINCIPLE OF MOMENTS & COUPLE

4.5 Principle of Moments

A body is in equilibrium if the sum of clockwise moments acting on the body is equal to the sum of anticlockwise moments.

Σ(Clockwise Moments) = Σ(Anticlockwise Moments)

4.6 Couple

Two equal and opposite forces acting along different lines of action form a couple. It produces pure rotation without translation.

Moment of Couple = F × d

Where F = Magnitude of one force and d = Couple arm (perpendicular distance between forces).

PAGE 4: CENTER OF MASS & CENTER OF GRAVITY

4.7 Center of Mass

The point where the entire mass of the body is considered to be concentrated.

4.8 Center of Gravity

The point where the whole weight of the body acts vertically downward.

For uniform objects, center of mass and center of gravity coincide. For regular shapes, it is at the geometric center. For irregular shapes, use the plumb line method.
PAGE 5: CONDITIONS OF EQUILIBRIUM

4.9 Equilibrium

A body is in equilibrium when it is at rest or moving with uniform velocity (no acceleration).

First Condition: The net force acting on a body is zero.

ΣF = 0 (ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0)

Second Condition: The sum of clockwise torques equals the sum of anticlockwise torques.

Στ = 0

CHAPTER 5: PROPERTIES OF MATTER - FORCES, ELASTICITY & PRESSURE

PAGE 1: DEFORMATION & ELASTICITY

1. DEFORMATION OF MATERIALS

Definition: When forces act on an object, they can change its shape or size. This change is called deformation.

Types of Deformation:

TypeDescriptionEffectExample
TensileForce pulls/stretch the objectObject becomes longerStretching a rubber band, pulling a spring
CompressiveForce pushes/squashes the objectObject becomes shorterSqueezing a sponge, compressing a spring

2. ELASTIC BEHAVIOR

Elastic Change: An object undergoes elastic change when it returns to its original shape and size after the load (force) is removed.

Examples: Rubber bands, Springs, Balloons.

Plastic Deformation: When an object does NOT return to its original shape after removing the load. Permanent deformation occurs.

PAGE 2: HOOKE'S LAW & EXTENSION GRAPHS

3. HOOKE'S LAW

Statement: "The extension in a spring is directly proportional to the load applied to it, provided the limit of elasticity is not exceeded."

F = kx

Where: F = Force (N); k = Spring constant (N/m); x = Extension (m).

Key Concepts:

TermDefinition
Spring Constant (k)Measure of stiffness. Higher k = Stiffer spring.
Limit of ProportionalityMaximum load up to which Hooke's law is obeyed.
Elastic LimitMaximum extension after which the spring won't return to original length.

4. EXTENSION-LOAD GRAPH

  • A straight line through origin represents Hooke's Law.
  • The gradient (slope) of the graph = Spring constant (k).
  • Beyond the limit of proportionality, the graph curves.
PAGE 3: PRESSURE IN SOLIDS

5. PRESSURE - BASIC CONCEPT

Definition: Pressure is the force acting per unit area on a surface.

P = F / A

Key Principle: "Pressure is greater when a large force acts on a small area"

Practical Examples:

  • Sharp knife cuts better: Small area = Higher pressure.
  • Wide tires: Large area reduces pressure on ground.
  • Nail pointed tip: Concentrates force for easy penetration.

Units: SI Unit is Pascal (Pa). 1 Pa = 1 N/m².

PAGE 4: PRESSURE IN FLUIDS

6. FACTORS AFFECTING FLUID PRESSURE

Pressure in a fluid is greater with large depth and high density.

7. FLUID PRESSURE FORMULA

P = ρgh

Where: ρ (rho) = Density, g = gravity, h = Depth.

Applications: Dam walls are thicker at the bottom to withstand higher pressure at greater depths.

PAGE 5: PASCAL'S LAW & HYDRAULIC SYSTEMS

8. PASCAL'S LAW

Statement: "Liquids transmit pressure equally in all directions"

When pressure is applied to a confined fluid, it is transmitted undiminished throughout the fluid.

9. HYDRAULIC MACHINES

Devices that multiply force using Pascal's Law.

F₂ / F₁ = A₂ / A₁

Applications: Hydraulic Brakes, Hydraulic Lifts, Hydraulic Jacks, Excavators.

Result: A small input force can lift a very heavy load if the output area is much larger than the input area.

CHAPTER 6: GRAVITATION - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

PAGE 1: NEWTON'S LAW OF GRAVITATION

6.1 Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

F = G (m₁m₂) / r²

Where G is the Universal Gravitational Constant: G = 6.673 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg².

PAGE 2: GRAVITATIONAL FIELD & MASS OF EARTH

6.2 Gravitational Field

The region around a mass where it exerts a gravitational force on other masses.

Field Strength (g): Force per unit mass at a point.

g = F / m = GM / R²

6.3 Mass of the Earth

By using Newton’s law and the value of 'g' at the surface:

M_e = gR_e² / G

Mass of Earth ≈ 6.0 × 10²⁴ kg.

PAGE 3: VARIATION OF 'G' & SATELLITES

6.4 Variation of 'g' with Altitude

The value of 'g' decreases as we go higher above the Earth's surface.

g_h = GM / (R+h)²

6.5 Artificial Satellites

Objects that revolve around the Earth. Their orbital velocity depends on their altitude.

v_o = √(g_h × (R+h))

Geostationary Satellites: Have an orbital period of 24 hours (same as Earth's rotation) and appear stationary over one point on the equator. Altitude ≈ 36,000 km.

PAGE 2: ARCHIMEDES' PRINCIPLE & FLOATATION

7.3 Archimedes’ Principle

When an object is immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward force (upthrust) equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces.

Upthrust = ρ × g × V_displaced

7.4 Principle of Floatation

A floating object displaces a weight of fluid equal to its own weight.

  • If Weight > Upthrust: Object Sinks.
  • If Weight = Upthrust: Object Floats.

7.5 Pascal's Law

Pressure applied at any point of a liquid enclosed in a container is transmitted without loss to all other parts of the liquid.

Application: Hydraulic Press (F₂/A₂ = F₁/A₁).

CHAPTER 8: THERMAL PROPERTIES OF MATTER - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

PAGE 1: TEMPERATURE, HEAT & SCALES

8.1 Temperature vs Heat

Temperature: Degree of hotness or coldness. (Average Kinetic Energy).

Heat: Form of energy transferred due to temperature difference. (Total Internal Energy).

8.2 Temperature Scales

  • Celsius (°C): Freezing 0°, Boiling 100°.
  • Kelvin (K): SI Unit. Absolute zero = -273.15°C.
  • Conversion: K = °C + 273
PAGE 2: SPECIFIC HEAT & LATENT HEAT

8.3 Specific Heat Capacity (c)

Amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

Q = mcΔT

Unit: J/kg·K. Water has high specific heat (4200 J/kg·K).

8.4 Latent Heat (L)

Heat absorbed or released during a phase change without change in temperature.

  • Fusion (L_f): Solid ↔ Liquid.
  • Vaporization (L_v): Liquid ↔ Gas.
Q = mL
PAGE 3: EVAPORATION

8.5 Evaporation

Change of liquid into vapors from the surface at any temperature below its boiling point.

Factors: Surface area, Temperature, Wind speed, Humidity.

Evaporation causes cooling because high energy molecules escape, leaving behind lower energy (cooler) molecules.

CHAPTER 9: TRANSFER OF HEAT - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

PAGE 1: MODES OF HEAT TRANSFER

9.1 Conduction

Transfer of heat through direct contact of molecules (mostly in solids).

Mechanism: Vibration of atoms and free electrons (in metals).

9.2 Convection

Transfer of heat by the actual movement of molecules (in liquids and gases).

Examples: Land and sea breezes, convection currents in room heaters.

9.3 Radiation

Transfer of heat in the form of electromagnetic waves. Does not require a medium.

Example: Heat from Sun reaching Earth.

PAGE 2: EMISSION, ABSORPTION & GREENHOUSE

9.4 Emission and Absorption

  • Dull Black Surfaces: Good absorbers and good emitters.
  • Shiny White Surfaces: Poor absorbers and poor emitters (good reflectors).

9.5 Greenhouse Effect

The trapping of solar radiation by gases (CO₂, Water vapor) in the atmosphere, keeping the Earth warm.

Global Warming: Increase in Earth's average temperature due to enhanced greenhouse effect.

9.6 Applications

  • Thermos Flask: Reduces heat loss by conduction (vacuum), convection (stopper), and radiation (silvered walls).
  • Cooking Utensils: Bottoms made of copper (good conductor), handles made of wood/plastic (insulator).
