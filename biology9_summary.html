CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION TO BIOLOGY

Definition & Major Branches

Biology: The scientific study of life and living organisms. Derived from Greek: Bios (life) + Logos (study/reasoning).

  • Zoology: Study of animals.
  • Botany: Study of plants.
  • Microbiology: Study of microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa).
  • Morphology: Study of external form and structure.
  • Anatomy: Study of internal structures.
  • Physiology: Study of functions of living parts.
  • Genetics: Study of genes, heredity, and variation.
  • Ecology: Study of interactions between organisms and their environment.
  • Taxonomy: Classification and naming of organisms.

Relationship with Other Sciences

  • Biophysics: Application of physics principles to biological phenomena (e.g., lever mechanics in limbs, fluid dynamics in blood flow).
  • Biochemistry: Study of chemical processes and substances in living organisms (e.g., photosynthesis, respiration).
  • Biometry (Biostatistics): Use of mathematical and statistical methods to analyze biological data.
  • Biogeography: Study of distribution of organisms across geographical regions.
  • Bio-economics: Economic valuation of biological resources and agricultural production.

Levels of Biological Organization

Life is organized hierarchically from simple to complex:

  1. Subatomic & Atomic: Protons, neutrons, electrons → Atoms (C, H, O, N).
  2. Molecular: Biomolecules (carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, nucleic acids).
  3. Organelle/Cellular: Functional units like mitochondria, nucleus → Cell (basic unit of life).
  4. Tissue → Organ → Organ System: Groups of similar cells → Structures with specific functions → Systems (e.g., digestive, nervous).
  5. Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biosphere: Individual → Group of same species → Multiple species interacting → Biotic + abiotic factors → Global ecological system.

CHAPTER 2: SOLVING A BIOLOGICAL PROBLEM

The Biological Method

A systematic approach to investigate and solve biological questions.

  1. Recognition of Biological Problem: Identifying a question or issue (e.g., "What causes malaria?").
  2. Observations: Gathering data. Qualitative (descriptive) vs Quantitative (numerical/measurable).
  3. Hypothesis: A tentative, testable explanation based on observations. Must be falsifiable.
  4. Deduction: Logical "if-then" predictions derived from the hypothesis.
  5. Experimentation: Controlled testing. Includes Control Group (normal conditions) and Experimental Group (variable changed).
  6. Variables: Independent (changed), Dependent (measured), Controlled (kept constant).
  7. Results & Conclusion: Data analysis leads to acceptance or rejection of the hypothesis.
  8. Reporting: Publication in scientific journals for peer review.

Theory vs Law

  • Theory: Well-substantiated explanation repeatedly confirmed through experimentation (e.g., Cell Theory, Germ Theory).
  • Law: Uniform or constant fact of nature describing what happens, not why (e.g., Mendel's Laws, Hardy-Weinberg Law).

Case Study: Malaria

Discovered by Charles Laveran (Plasmodium in blood), proven by Ronald Ross (mosquito vector), and later confirmed by Grassi (Anopheles species). Treatment includes Quinine and Artemisinin.

CHAPTER 3: BIODIVERSITY

Biodiversity & Classification

Biodiversity: The variety of life forms at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels.

Classification: Grouping organisms based on similarities/differences for systematic study.

Binomial Nomenclature: System by Carolus Linnaeus. Scientific name = Genus (capitalized) + species (lowercase), italicized (e.g., Homo sapiens).

Taxonomic Hierarchy

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. Species is the basic taxonomic unit.

Five Kingdom System (R.H. Whittaker, 1969)

  • Monera: Prokaryotic, unicellular (Bacteria, Cyanobacteria).
  • Protista: Eukaryotic, mostly unicellular (Amoeba, Paramecium, Algae).
  • Fungi: Eukaryotic, multicellular/unicellular, absorptive heterotrophs, chitin cell walls (Yeast, Mushrooms).
  • Plantae: Eukaryotic, multicellular, autotrophic, cellulose cell walls.
  • Animalia: Eukaryotic, multicellular, ingestive heterotrophs, no cell walls.

Conservation of Biodiversity

Threats: Habitat loss, deforestation, pollution, overhunting, climate change, invasive species.

Conservation Methods: Protected areas (National Parks, Sanctuaries), Captive breeding, Seed banks, CITES treaty, Reforestation.

Endangered Species (Pakistan): Indus Dolphin, Snow Leopard, Markhor, Houbara Bustard, Blind Indus River Dolphin.

CHAPTER 4: CELLS AND TISSUES

Microscopy & Cell Theory

  • Light Microscope: Uses visible light, magnifies up to 1500X, resolution ~0.2 μm.
  • Electron Microscope: Uses electron beams. TEM (internal structure), SEM (3D surface). Resolution up to 0.2 nm.
  • Cell Theory: 1. All living things are made of cells. 2. Cell is the basic unit of life. 3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells (Virchow).

Cell Structure & Organelles

  • Cell Membrane: Selectively permeable, Fluid Mosaic Model (phospholipid bilayer + proteins).
  • Cell Wall: Rigid outer layer in plants (cellulose), fungi (chitin), bacteria (peptidoglycan).
  • Nucleus: Control center, contains DNA/chromosomes, nuclear envelope with pores.
  • Mitochondria: Powerhouse, site of aerobic respiration, double membrane, cristae.
  • Chloroplasts: Site of photosynthesis, contains thylakoids & stroma, chlorophyll.
  • ER: RER (protein synthesis, ribosomes attached), SER (lipid synthesis, detoxification).
  • Golgi Apparatus: Modifies, packages, and ships proteins/lipids.
  • Ribosomes: Protein synthesis (free in cytoplasm or on RER).
  • Lysosomes: Contain digestive enzymes, waste disposal, autophagy.
  • Vacuoles: Storage (large central vacuole in plants for turgor pressure).

Transport Across Membrane

  • Passive Transport: Diffusion, Osmosis (water movement across semi-permeable membrane), Facilitated Diffusion (carrier proteins).
  • Active Transport: Requires ATP, moves substances against concentration gradient (e.g., Na+/K+ pump).

Tissues

  • Animal Tissues: Epithelial (covering/lining), Connective (bone, blood, cartilage), Muscular (skeletal, smooth, cardiac), Nervous (neurons, glial cells).
  • Plant Tissues: Meristematic (apical, lateral - cell division), Permanent (Simple: parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma; Complex: xylem, phloem).

CHAPTER 5: CELL CYCLE

Phases of Cell Cycle

  • Interphase (90% of cycle): G1 (growth, organelle duplication), S (DNA replication, chromosome duplication), G2 (preparation for division, protein synthesis).
  • M Phase: Mitosis or Meiosis followed by Cytokinesis (cytoplasm division).

Mitosis

Equational division: One parent cell → Two genetically identical daughter cells (2n → 2n).

  1. Prophase: Chromatin condenses into chromosomes, nuclear envelope breaks, spindle fibers form.
  2. Metaphase: Chromosomes align at equatorial plate (metaphase plate).
  3. Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate, move to opposite poles.
  4. Telophase: Nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense, cytokinesis begins.

Significance: Growth, tissue repair, asexual reproduction, replacement of dead cells.

Meiosis

Reduction division: One diploid cell → Four haploid gametes (2n → n). Occurs in gonads.

  • Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes pair (synapsis), cross over (genetic variation), separate into two cells.
  • Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate (similar to mitosis).
  • Key Events: Crossing over (Prophase I), Independent assortment (Metaphase I) → Genetic diversity.

Significance: Gamete formation, maintains chromosome number across generations, introduces variation.

Apoptosis vs Necrosis

  • Apoptosis: Programmed, controlled cell death. Removes damaged/unnecessary cells. Beneficial.
  • Necrosis: Uncontrolled cell death due to injury, toxins, or infection. Causes inflammation. Harmful.

CHAPTER 6: ENZYMES

Definition & Characteristics

Enzymes: Biological catalysts (mostly globular proteins) that speed up chemical reactions by lowering activation energy without being consumed.

  • Highly specific to their substrate.
  • Reversible action; reusable.
  • Named with suffix -ase (e.g., Amylase, Lipase, Protease).
  • Require optimal temperature and pH.

Mechanism of Action

  • Active Site: Specific region where substrate binds.
  • Lock & Key Model: Rigid active site perfectly fits substrate.
  • Induced Fit Model: Active site changes shape slightly to mold around substrate (more accurate).
  • Forms Enzyme-Substrate (ES) complex → Enzyme-Product (EP) complex → Products released.

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

  • Temperature: Rate increases with temp up to optimum (~37°C in humans). High temp causes denaturation (loss of 3D shape).
  • pH: Each enzyme has an optimum pH (Pepsin: pH 2, Trypsin: pH 8). Extreme pH denatures enzymes.
  • Substrate Concentration: Rate increases until all active sites are saturated (Vmax).
  • Inhibitors: Competitive (blocks active site, reversible), Non-competitive (binds elsewhere, alters shape, irreversible).
  • Cofactors/Coenzymes: Non-protein helpers (e.g., Metal ions like Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺; Vitamins like B-complex).

CHAPTER 7: BIOENERGETICS

ATP: The Energy Currency

Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP): Composed of adenine, ribose, and 3 phosphate groups. High-energy bonds between phosphates store energy. Hydrolysis: ATP → ADP + Pi + Energy.

Photosynthesis

Process by which autotrophs convert light energy into chemical energy (glucose).

Equation: 6CO₂ + 12H₂O + Light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ + 6H₂O

  • Light-Dependent Reactions: Occur in thylakoid membranes. Light splits water (photolysis), produces ATP & NADPH, releases O₂.
  • Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle): Occur in stroma. Uses ATP & NADPH to fix CO₂ into glucose.
  • Factors Affecting Rate: Light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature, water availability.

Cellular Respiration

Breakdown of glucose to release ATP. Occurs in mitochondria (and cytoplasm).

  • Aerobic Respiration (with O₂): 3 stages: Glycolysis (cytoplasm, glucose → pyruvate, 2 ATP), Krebs Cycle (mitochondrial matrix, CO₂ release, electron carriers), Electron Transport Chain (inner membrane, produces ~34 ATP, H₂O formed). Total: ~36-38 ATP.
  • Anaerobic Respiration (without O₂): Alcoholic Fermentation (yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP). Lactic Acid Fermentation (muscle cells: glucose → lactic acid + 2 ATP). Causes muscle fatigue.

Comparison: Photosynthesis stores energy (anabolic), Respiration releases energy (catabolic). They are complementary processes.

CHAPTER 8: NUTRITION

Modes of Nutrition

  • Autotrophic: Synthesize own food (Photosynthesis/Chemosynthesis).
  • Heterotrophic: Depend on others. Types: Holozoic (ingestion), Saprophytic (dead matter), Parasitic (host).

Human Digestive System

Alimentary Canal: Mouth → Pharynx → Esophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine → Large Intestine → Rectum → Anus.

  • Mouth: Mechanical (teeth) & chemical digestion (salivary amylase → starch → maltose).
  • Stomach: Gastric juice contains HCl (kills bacteria, activates pepsin) & Pepsin (protein → peptides). Mucus protects lining.
  • Small Intestine: Duodenum (receives bile from liver, pancreatic juice), Jejunum & Ileum (absorption). Villi & microvilli increase surface area.
  • Large Intestine: Absorbs water & salts, forms feces. Contains symbiotic bacteria (produce Vitamin K & B).

Digestive Glands & Enzymes

  • Salivary Glands: Amylase.
  • Liver: Produces bile (emulsifies fats, neutralizes acid). Stores glycogen, vitamins.
  • Pancreas: Amylase (starch), Trypsin (proteins), Lipase (fats). Bicarbonate neutralizes chyme.
  • Gastric Glands: Pepsin, HCl.

Nutritional Disorders

  • Malnutrition: Deficiency/excess of nutrients (e.g., Kwashiorkor, Marasmus, Obesity).
  • Peptic Ulcer: Erosion of stomach/duodenum lining (H. pylori, stress, spicy food).
  • Constipation/Diarrhea: Irregular bowel movements due to diet, infection, or dehydration.

CHAPTER 9: TRANSPORT

Transport in Plants

  • Xylem: Transports water & minerals upward. Made of tracheids & vessel elements. Mechanism: Transpiration Pull (cohesion-tension theory), root pressure.
  • Phloem: Transports organic food (sucrose) bidirectionally. Made of sieve tubes & companion cells. Mechanism: Pressure Flow Hypothesis (Münch).
  • Transpiration: Water loss as vapor through stomata. Regulated by guard cells (K⁺ ion movement). Factors: Light, temp, humidity, wind.

Human Circulatory System

  • Blood Components: Plasma (90% water, nutrients, hormones, waste), RBCs (hemoglobin, O₂ transport, biconcave, no nucleus), WBCs (immunity: phagocytes, lymphocytes), Platelets (clotting).
  • Blood Groups: ABO system (A, B, AB, O) & Rh factor (+/-). Universal donor: O-, Universal recipient: AB+.
  • Heart: Muscular pump with 4 chambers. Right side (deoxygenated → lungs), Left side (oxygenated → body). Valves prevent backflow. Cardiac cycle: Systole (contraction) & Diastole (relaxation).
  • Blood Vessels: Arteries (thick walls, away from heart, high pressure), Veins (thin walls, towards heart, valves), Capillaries (exchange site, one cell thick).
  • Double Circulation: Pulmonary circuit (heart ↔ lungs) + Systemic circuit (heart ↔ body).

Transport Disorders

  • Hypertension: High blood pressure (>140/90 mmHg). Risk factor for stroke/heart attack.
  • Atherosclerosis: Plaque (fat/cholesterol) buildup in arteries, narrowing lumen.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Heart attack due to blocked coronary artery.
  • Leukemia: Cancer of WBCs (uncontrolled production).
  • Anemia: Low RBCs/hemoglobin → fatigue, weakness.
