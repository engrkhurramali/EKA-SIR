CHAPTER 10: GASEOUS EXCHANGE

10.1 Gaseous Exchange in Plants

Definition: The exchange of gases (Oxygen and Carbon Dioxide) between an organism and its environment.

Plants exchange gases through:

  • Stomata: Tiny pores in leaves surrounded by guard cells. They open during the day for photosynthesis.
  • Lenticels: Small openings in the bark of stems and roots of older plants.

10.2 Human Respiratory System

The pathway of air: Nasal Cavity → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli.

  • Lungs: Paired organs where gaseous exchange occurs. Left lung has 2 lobes, Right lung has 3.
  • Alveoli: Microscopic air sacs where blood capillaries exchange O₂ and CO₂ via diffusion.
  • Breathing Mechanism: Involves the contraction and relaxation of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles.

10.3 Respiratory Disorders

  • Bronchitis: Inflammation of bronchi caused by viruses or bacteria.
  • Emphysema: Destruction of alveolar walls, reducing surface area for gas exchange (often caused by smoking).
  • Pneumonia: Infection of lung tissues, usually caused by bacteria.
  • Asthma: Inflammation and narrowing of airways causing difficulty in breathing.

CHAPTER 11: HOMEOSTASIS

11.1 Concept of Homeostasis

Definition: The maintenance of a constant internal environment of the body. This includes temperature regulation, water balance, and salt balance.

11.2 Osmoregulation

Maintenance of water and salt balance in body fluids. In humans, this is primarily performed by the kidneys.

11.3 Human Urinary System

  • Kidneys: Bean-shaped organs that filter blood to remove urea, salts, and excess water.
  • Ureters: Tubes carrying urine from kidneys to the bladder.
  • Urinary Bladder: Sac-like structure that stores urine.
  • Urethra: Tube through which urine is expelled from the body.

Nephron: The structural and functional unit of the kidney. It consists of the renal corpuscle (Bowman's capsule + Glomerulus) and renal tubule.

11.4 Kidney Disorders

  • Kidney Stones: Hard deposits of minerals and salts that form inside the kidneys.
  • Kidney Failure: When kidneys can no longer filter waste, treated by Dialysis or Transplant.

CHAPTER 12: COORDINATION AND CONTROL

12.1 Types of Coordination

  • Nervous Coordination: Fast, electrical signals transmitted by the Nervous System.
  • Chemical Coordination: Slower, chemical signals transmitted by Hormones (Endocrine System).

12.2 Human Nervous System

Neuron: The basic unit of the nervous system consisting of Cell Body, Dendrites, and Axon.

Central Nervous System (CNS): Brain and Spinal Cord.

  • Forebrain: Cerebrum (Thinking, voluntary actions), Thalamus, Hypothalamus (Homeostasis center).
  • Hindbrain: Cerebellum (Balance/Coordination), Medulla Oblongata (Involuntary functions like heartbeat).

12.3 Reflex Action

An automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus (e.g., pulling hand from fire). The pathway is called the Reflex Arc.

12.4 Endocrine System

Ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the blood.

  • Pituitary Gland: Master gland (Growth hormone, etc.).
  • Thyroid: Thyroxin (Metabolism).
  • Adrenal: Adrenaline (Fight or flight response).
  • Pancreas: Insulin (Lowers blood sugar) and Glucagon (Raises blood sugar).

CHAPTER 13: SUPPORT AND MOVEMENT

13.1 Human Skeleton

Provides support, protection, and aids movement. It consists of 206 bones.

  • Axial Skeleton: Skull, vertebral column, ribs (Protects CNS and organs).
  • Appendicular Skeleton: Pectoral girdle, Pelvic girdle, Limbs (Aids locomotion).

13.2 Bone and Cartilage

  • Bone: Hard, non-flexible connective tissue with calcium deposits.
  • Cartilage: Soft, flexible connective tissue found at joints and nose.

13.3 Joints

The point where two or more bones meet.

  • Fibrous Joints: Immovable (Skull sutures).
  • Cartilaginous Joints: Slightly movable (Vertebrae).
  • Synovial Joints: Freely movable (Knee, Elbow). Types include Hinge, Ball-and-Socket, Pivot.

13.4 Muscles

Muscles work in antagonistic pairs (one contracts while the other relaxes) to move bones. E.g., Biceps and Triceps.

CHAPTER 14: REPRODUCTION

14.1 Asexual Reproduction

Involves one parent; offspring are genetically identical. Common in plants and lower animals.

  • Methods: Binary Fission, Budding, Spore Formation, Fragmentation, Vegetative Propagation (Cuttings, Grafting).

14.2 Sexual Reproduction in Plants

  • Pollination: Transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma. Types: Self-pollination, Cross-pollination (Wind/Insect).
  • Fertilization: Fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote.

14.3 Human Reproduction

Male System: Testes produce sperm. Female System: Ovaries produce eggs. Ovulation: Release of an egg from the ovary.

Fertilization occurs in the oviduct (Fallopian tube). The developing embryo implants in the uterus.

14.4 Reproductive Health

  • STDs: Sexually Transmitted Diseases (e.g., AIDS, Hepatitis, Syphilis).
  • Family Planning: Methods to control birth rates (Contraceptives).

CHAPTER 15: INHERITANCE

15.1 Genetics and Mendel

Gregor Mendel: Father of Genetics. He worked on pea plants.

  • Gene: Unit of heredity controlling a trait.
  • Allele: Alternative form of a gene.
  • Genotype: Genetic makeup (e.g., TT, Tt, tt).
  • Phenotype: Physical appearance (e.g., Tall, Dwarf).
  • Dominant: Trait that masks the other (Tall).
  • Recessive: Trait that is masked (Dwarf).

15.2 Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation: Alleles separate during gamete formation.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits are inherited independently.

15.3 Sex Determination

Humans have 46 chromosomes. Sex is determined by the 23rd pair (XX = Female, XY = Male). Since males produce X and Y sperm, the father determines the sex of the child.

15.4 Co-Dominance

When neither allele is dominant, and both are expressed (e.g., Blood group AB).

CHAPTER 16: MAN AND HIS ENVIRONMENT

16.1 Ecosystem Components

  • Abiotic Factors: Non-living (Light, Water, Temperature, Soil).
  • Biotic Factors: Living organisms classified as:
    • Producers: Autotrophs (Plants) making food via photosynthesis.
    • Consumers: Heterotrophs (Herbivores, Carnivores, Omnivores).
    • Decomposers: Bacteria and Fungi that break down dead matter.

16.2 Flow of Energy

  • Food Chain: Linear sequence of feeding (Grass → Rabbit → Fox).
  • Food Web: Interconnected food chains.
  • Pyramid of Biomass: Energy decreases as we move up trophic levels.

16.3 Biogeochemical Cycles

  • Water Cycle: Evaporation, Transpiration, Condensation, Precipitation.
  • Carbon Cycle: Carbon moves between atmosphere (CO₂), plants, animals, and soil.
  • Nitrogen Cycle: Nitrogen fixation (by bacteria), Nitrification, Denitrification.

16.4 Ecological Issues

Pollution (Air, Water, Soil), Deforestation, Global Warming (Greenhouse effect due to increased CO₂ and Methane), and Acid Rain.

CHAPTER 17: BIOTECHNOLOGY

17.1 Introduction

Definition: The use of living organisms or biological systems to create useful products for humans.

Traditional Biotech: Fermentation (Yogurt, Bread, Alcohol). Modern Biotech: Genetic Engineering.

17.2 Fermentation

An anaerobic process by microbes to convert carbohydrates into alcohol or acids. Used in making cheese, yogurt, vinegar, and bread.

17.3 Genetic Engineering

Techniques to alter DNA.

  • Recombinant DNA: DNA formed by combining genetic material from different sources.
  • Transgenic Organisms: Organisms containing foreign DNA (e.g., Golden Rice, Bt Cotton, Transgenic mice).

17.4 Single Cell Protein (SCP)

Protein-rich food produced from microorganisms like algae, bacteria, and fungi. High in protein and vitamins; used to combat malnutrition.

17.5 Applications in Medicine

Production of Insulin (for diabetics), Growth Hormone, and Hepatitis vaccines using genetically modified bacteria.

CHAPTER 18: PHARMACOLOGY

18.1 Medicinal Drugs

Definition: A substance used to treat, cure, or prevent diseases. Sources include Plants, Animals, Minerals, and Synthetics.

18.2 Addictive Drugs

Drugs that cause physical or psychological dependence.

  • Narcotics: Painkillers derived from Opium (Morphine, Heroin). Highly addictive, cause drowsiness and respiratory depression.
  • Sedatives: Depress the central nervous system to induce sleep (Barbiturates, Diazepam).

18.3 Antibiotics

Medicines that kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria.

  • Broad Spectrum: Effective against many types of bacteria.
  • Narrow Spectrum: Effective against specific bacteria.
  • Example: Penicillin (from Penicillium fungus), Cephalosporins.

18.4 Vaccines

Definition: Biological preparation that provides active acquired immunity to a particular infectious disease. It contains a killed or weakened form of the germ.

Examples: Polio vaccine, BCG (Tuberculosis), Measles vaccine, Hepatitis B vaccine.

18.5 Drug Misuse

Using drugs inappropriately without prescription or taking overdose leads to toxicity and organ failure.
