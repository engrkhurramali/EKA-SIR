CHAPTER 1: FUNDAMENTALS OF CHEMISTRY

Basic Definitions

  • Matter: Anything that has mass and occupies space.
  • Substance: A piece of matter in pure form.
  • Mixture: Impure matter containing two or more substances.
  • Element: A substance made of same type of atoms, having same atomic number and cannot be decomposed into simple substances.
  • Compound: A substance made of two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio by mass.

Atomic Number & Mass Number

Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.

Mass Number (A): The sum of the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

A = Z + n

Empirical vs Molecular Formula

  • Empirical Formula: Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms present in a compound.
  • Molecular Formula: Shows the actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule.

Mole & Avogadro's Number

Avogadro's Number (NA): 6.022 x 1023 particles per mole.

Mole: The amount of substance that contains Avogadro's number of particles.

Number of Moles = Mass / Molar Mass

CHAPTER 2: ATOMIC STRUCTURE

Early Theories

  • Dalton's Atomic Theory: Atoms are indivisible, hard, dense spheres.
  • Rutherford's Model: Atoms have a small, dense, positively charged nucleus. Electrons revolve around it.
  • Bohr's Atomic Theory: Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits called energy levels or shells.

Subatomic Particles

  • Electrons: Negatively charged, discovered by J.J. Thomson (Cathode Rays).
  • Protons: Positively charged, discovered by Goldstein (Canal Rays).
  • Neutrons: Neutral, discovered by James Chadwick in 1932.

Isotopes

Atoms of the same element having the same atomic number but different mass numbers (due to different number of neutrons).

Examples: Protium, Deuterium, Tritium (Isotopes of Hydrogen).

Uses: Radiotherapy (Co-60), Carbon dating (C-14).

CHAPTER 3: PERIODIC TABLE & PERIODICITY

Evolution of Periodic Table

  • Dobereiner's Triads: Groups of 3 elements with similar properties.
  • Newlands' Octaves: Every 8th element had similar properties.
  • Mendeleev's Periodic Table: Arranged elements based on increasing atomic masses.
  • Modern Periodic Table: Arranged elements based on increasing atomic numbers (Moseley).

Structure of Periodic Table

  • Periods: Horizontal rows (7 in total).
  • Groups: Vertical columns (18 in total). Elements in a group have similar valence shell configurations.

Periodic Trends

  • Atomic Size: Decreases across a period, increases down a group.
  • Ionization Energy: Energy required to remove an electron. Increases across a period, decreases down a group.
  • Electronegativity: Ability of an atom to attract shared pair of electrons. Increases across a period, decreases down a group.

CHAPTER 4: STRUCTURE OF MOLECULES

Chemical Bonding

Atoms bond to achieve stability by attaining the electronic configuration of noble gases (Octet/Duplet rule).

Types of Bonds

  • Ionic Bond: Formed by complete transfer of electrons (e.g., NaCl).
  • Covalent Bond: Formed by mutual sharing of electrons (e.g., H2, O2).
  • Dative (Coordinate) Covalent Bond: One atom provides both electrons for the shared pair.
  • Metallic Bond: Formed due to attraction between metal cations and mobile electrons.

Intermolecular Forces

Forces of attraction between molecules (e.g., Dipole-dipole interactions, Hydrogen bonding).

Hydrogen Bonding: Special dipole-dipole attraction between H and highly electronegative atoms (N, O, F).

CHAPTER 5: PHYSICAL STATES OF MATTER

Gaseous State

  • Diffusion: Spontaneous mixing of gas molecules.
  • Effusion: Escape of gas through a tiny hole.
  • Boyle's Law: V \u221d 1/P (at constant T). P1V1 = P2V2.
  • Charles's Law: V \u221d T (at constant P). V1/T1 = V2/T2.

Liquid State

  • Evaporation: Cooling process, conversion of liquid to gas at surface.
  • Vapor Pressure: Pressure exerted by vapors in equilibrium with liquid.
  • Boiling Point: Temperature at which vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure.

Solid State

  • Melting Point: Temperature at which solid and liquid coexist in equilibrium.
  • Allotropy: Existence of an element in more than one physical form (e.g., Carbon as Diamond and Graphite).

CHAPTER 6: SOLUTIONS

Types of Solutions

  • Saturated: Contains maximum amount of solute at a given temperature.
  • Unsaturated: Can dissolve more solute.
  • Supersaturated: More concentrated than saturated solution.

Concentration Units

  • Percentage (%): Mass/Mass, Mass/Volume, Volume/Mass, Volume/Volume.
  • Molarity (M): Number of moles of solute per liter of solution.
Molarity = Moles of solute / Liters of solution

Solubility

The amount of solute in grams that dissolves in 100g of solvent at a specific temperature. "Like dissolves like" is the general rule.

CHAPTER 7: ELECTROCHEMISTRY

Redox Reactions

  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons or addition of oxygen.
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons or addition of hydrogen.

Electrochemical Cells

  • Electrolytic Cell: Uses electricity to drive a non-spontaneous reaction (e.g., Nelson's cell).
  • Galvanic (Voltaic) Cell: Uses spontaneous chemical reactions to produce electricity (e.g., Daniel cell).

Corrosion

Slow eating away of metal by environment. Rusting of Iron requires both oxygen and water.

Prevention: Painting, Greasing, Galvanizing, Electroplating.

CHAPTER 8: CHEMICAL REACTIVITY

Metals

Electropositive elements that lose electrons to form cations. Properties include luster, malleability, ductility, and high conductivity.

  • Alkali Metals (Group 1): Highly reactive, soft.
  • Alkaline Earth Metals (Group 2): Reactive, harder than group 1.

Non-metals

Electronegative elements that gain electrons to form anions. Poor conductors of heat and electricity.

Halogens (Group 17): Very reactive non-metals (F, Cl, Br, I).

Noble Gases (Group 18): Non-reactive due to complete valence shells.
