CHAPTER 10: SIMPLE HARMONIC MOTION AND WAVES

1. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

A periodic motion where the restoring acceleration is directly proportional to displacement and always directed toward the mean position[cite: 1].

  • Hooke's Law: F = -kx. The negative sign indicates the force is restorative (opposing displacement).
  • Mass-Spring System: Time Period T = 2π√(m/k). Restoring force is provided by the elasticity of the spring.
  • Simple Pendulum: Time Period T = 2π√(l/g). Restoring force is the component of gravity (mg sinθ). Note that mass does not affect the time period of a pendulum.
  • Kinematics of SHM: At the mean position, velocity is maximum and acceleration is zero. At extreme positions, velocity is zero and acceleration is maximum.
  • Damped Oscillations: The gradual reduction of amplitude of an oscillator due to resistive forces (like friction). Example: Shock absorbers in a car.
a ∝ -x   |   T = 2π√(m/k)   |   T = 2π√(l/g)

2. Wave Motion

A disturbance that transfers energy through matter or space without net matter transport[cite: 1].

  • Mechanical Waves: Require a material medium to propagate (e.g., sound waves, water waves)[cite: 1].
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require any medium; can travel through a vacuum (e.g., light, X-rays, radio waves)[cite: 1].
  • Transverse Waves: Particle vibration is perpendicular (⊥) to the wave's direction of propagation. Composed of crests (highest points) and troughs (lowest points).
  • Longitudinal Waves: Particle vibration is parallel (||) to the wave's direction. Composed of compressions (particles close together) and rarefactions (particles spread apart).
v = fλ   (Wave Equation)

3. Properties of Waves (Ripple Tank)

The Ripple Tank is an experimental device used to study water wave characteristics.

  • Reflection: When waves hit a barrier, they bounce back at the same angle (angle of incidence = angle of reflection)[cite: 1].
  • Refraction: Waves change speed and direction when moving from deep to shallow water (wavelength decreases, frequency remains constant)[cite: 1].
  • Diffraction: The bending of waves around the edges of an obstacle or slit. Most prominent when slit width is comparable to the wavelength[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 11: SOUND

1. Nature & Propagation of Sound

Sound is a mechanical longitudinal wave produced by vibrating bodies[cite: 1]. It propagates outward in all directions via alternating regions of compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure)[cite: 1]. It absolutely requires a material medium and cannot travel in a vacuum[cite: 1].

2. Characteristics of Sound

  • Loudness: The subjective perception of sound magnitude[cite: 1]. It depends on the amplitude of the vibrating body, surface area, and distance from the source.
  • Intensity of Sound: The acoustic energy passing per second through unit area held perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Unit: Watt per square meter (W/m²).
  • Sound Intensity Level: Measured in decibels (dB). β = 10 log(I / I₀), where I₀ is the faintest audible sound (10⁻¹² W/m²).
  • Pitch: The characteristic that distinguishes a shrill sound from a grave one[cite: 1]. It depends strictly on frequency. (e.g., Women and children have higher pitch than adult men).
  • Quality (Timbre): Distinguishes two sounds of the same loudness and pitch due to the difference in their complex waveforms[cite: 1].

3. Speed of Sound

The speed of sound depends on the elasticity and inertia of the medium.

  • By Phase: Fastest in solids (highest elasticity), slower in liquids, and slowest in gases (vsolid > vliquid > vgas)[cite: 1].
  • Temperature Effect: Speed increases with an increase in temperature (v ∝ √T)[cite: 1]. In air at 0°C, v ≈ 332 m/s; at 20°C, v ≈ 340 m/s[cite: 1].
s = vt   →   v = s/t

4. Echo, Reverberation, & Acoustics

  • Echo: A distinct reflected sound[cite: 1]. For the human ear to distinguish an echo, the time delay must be ≥ 0.1s. Thus, the minimum distance to a reflecting surface must be ≈ 17m.
  • Reverberation: Multiple reflections of sound in a closed room causing the sound to persist[cite: 1]. Managed via acoustic materials (porous boards, heavy curtains).

5. Audible Range and Ultrasound

  • Audible Range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz for the average healthy human ear[cite: 1].
  • Ultrasound (Ultrasonics): Frequencies > 20,000 Hz[cite: 1]. Applications include SONAR (Sound Navigation and Ranging) to measure ocean depth, medical diagnostics (fetal imaging), removing kidney stones, and industrial flaw detection[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 12: GEOMETRICAL OPTICS

1. Reflection & Spherical Mirrors

Reflection is the bouncing back of light into the same medium. Laws of Reflection: 1. Angle of incidence (i) = Angle of reflection (r). 2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane[cite: 1].

  • Concave Mirror: Converging mirror. The inner curved surface is reflecting. Can form real/inverted images (if object is beyond principal focus F) or virtual/erect/magnified images (if object is inside F)[cite: 1].
  • Convex Mirror: Diverging mirror. The outer curved surface is reflecting. Always forms virtual, upright, and diminished images. Used as rear-view mirrors due to a wider field of view[cite: 1].
  • Mirror Formula & Magnification: Relates focal length (f), object distance (p), and image distance (q). f is positive for concave and negative for convex.
1/f = 1/p + 1/q   |   M = q/p = hi / ho

2. Refraction & Snell's Law

The bending of light as it passes from one transparent medium to another due to a change in speed[cite: 1]. Light bends toward the normal when entering a denser medium, and away from the normal when entering a rarer medium[cite: 1].

  • Snell's Law: The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is a constant called the Refractive Index (n).
  • Refractive Index: n = sin(i) / sin(r). Also, n = (Speed of light in vacuum c) / (Speed of light in medium v)[cite: 1].

3. Total Internal Reflection (TIR) & Optical Fibers

When light travels from a denser to a rarer medium and the angle of incidence exceeds the Critical Angle (C), no refraction occurs; all light is reflected back[cite: 1].

  • Critical Angle Formula: sin C = 1/n[cite: 1].
  • Applications: Optical fibers (telecommunications), total reflecting prisms (periscopes, binoculars), and mirages (optical illusion in deserts)[cite: 1].

4. Lenses & Defects of Vision

  • Convex Lens: Thicker at the center. Converges parallel light rays to a real focus.
  • Concave Lens: Thinner at the center. Diverges light rays from a virtual focus.
  • Power of a Lens: Reciprocal of focal length in meters. P = 1/f(in m). Unit: Diopter (D).
  • Myopia (Short-sightedness): Cannot see distant objects. Image forms in front of the retina. Corrected using a Concave lens[cite: 1].
  • Hypermetropia (Long-sightedness): Cannot see close objects. Image forms behind the retina. Corrected using a Convex lens[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 13: ELECTROSTATICS

1. Electric Charge & Electrostatic Induction

Charge is a fundamental property of matter. Like charges repel, unlike charges attract[cite: 1]. Charge is conserved in isolated systems[cite: 1].

  • Electrostatic Induction: The temporary redistribution of charges in an object caused by the presence of a nearby charged body, without any physical contact.
  • Gold Leaf Electroscope: A sensitive instrument used to detect the presence of charge, identify the nature of the charge, and test the nature of conductors/insulators.

2. Coulomb's Law

The electrostatic force of attraction or repulsion between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers[cite: 1].

F = k·(q&sub1;q&sub2;)/r²   (where k = 9×10&sup9; Nm²/C² in air/vacuum)[cite: 1]

3. Electric Field & Potential

  • Electric Field (E): The region around a charge where it exerts an electrostatic force on another charge. Field intensity E = F/q₀. Unit: N/C[cite: 1].
  • Electric Field Lines: Imaginary lines indicating the direction of the field. They originate from positive charges and end on negative charges. They never intersect[cite: 1].
  • Electric Potential (V): The work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to a point in an electric field. V = W/q. Unit: Volt (V)[cite: 1].

4. Capacitors & Dielectrics

A device used to store electric charge and electrical energy[cite: 1]. Consists of two parallel metallic plates separated by an insulator (dielectric).

  • Capacitance (C): C = Q/V. Unit: Farad (F). Depends on plate area, distance between plates, and the dielectric material[cite: 1].
  • Capacitors in Parallel: Potential difference is the same across all. Equivalent Capacitance Ceq = C&sub1; + C&sub2; + ...[cite: 1].
  • Capacitors in Series: Charge is the same on all plates. Equivalent Capacitance 1/Ceq = 1/C&sub1; + 1/C&sub2; + ...[cite: 1].
  • Energy Stored: U = ½CV² = ½QV[cite: 1].

5. Applications & Hazards of Static Electricity

Applications: Photocopiers (Xerography), electrostatic air precipitators (to clean factory smoke), and car painting.
Hazards: Lightning strikes, fires during refueling due to sparks. Mitigated by proper earthing and lightning conductors[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 14: CURRENT ELECTRICITY

1. Electric Current & Electromotive Force

  • Electric Current (I): The rate of flow of electric charges through a cross-section of a conductor. I = Q/t. Unit: Ampere (A)[cite: 1].
  • Conventional Current: Flowing from high potential (+ terminal) to low potential (- terminal), equivalent to the flow of positive charges[cite: 1].
  • Electromotive Force (EMF): The energy supplied by a battery/cell to move a unit positive charge completely around the circuit. EMF = W/Q. Unit: Volt (V)[cite: 1].

2. Ohm's Law & Resistance

The current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends, provided the physical state and temperature remain constant[cite: 1].

  • Formula: V = IR[cite: 1].
  • Ohmic vs Non-Ohmic: Ohmic conductors (metals) obey Ohm's law (linear V-I graph). Non-ohmic conductors (filament bulbs, thermistors, semiconductor diodes) do not[cite: 1].
  • Resistance (R) Factors: Resistance opposes current flow. R is directly proportional to length (L) and inversely proportional to cross-sectional area (A). R = ρ(L/A), where ρ is resistivity (specific resistance)[cite: 1].

3. Resistors in Series and Parallel

  • Series Combination: Current remains the same, voltage divides. Req = R&sub1; + R&sub2; + R&sub3;[cite: 1].
  • Parallel Combination: Voltage remains the same, current divides. 1/Req = 1/R&sub1; + 1/R&sub2; + 1/R&sub3;[cite: 1].

4. Electrical Energy & Joule's Law

Joule's Law: The heat generated in a resistor is directly proportional to the square of the current, the resistance, and the time. W = I²Rt.

  • Electric Power (P): Rate of doing electrical work. P = VI = I²R = V²/R. Unit: Watt (W)[cite: 1].
  • Commercial Unit (Kilowatt-hour): The energy consumed by a 1000W appliance acting for 1 hour. 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁲ Joules. Used for calculating electricity bills[cite: 1].

5. Electrical Hazards & Safety

Hazards include short circuits (low resistance paths drawing huge current) and overloading. Safety devices: Fuses (melt on excess current), MCBs (Miniature Circuit Breakers), Earthing wires (drain leakage current to the ground), and double insulation[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 15: ELECTROMAGNETISM

1. Magnetic Effects of Steady Current

Oersted discovered that an electric current produces a magnetic field[cite: 1].

  • Straight Wire: Magnetic field lines are concentric circles. Direction found by the Right-Hand Grip Rule: Point thumb in direction of conventional current, curled fingers show magnetic field direction[cite: 1].
  • Solenoid: A coil of many turns. When current flows, it acts like a bar magnet. The field inside is strong and uniform. Strength increases by increasing current (I) or number of turns (n)[cite: 1].

2. Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor & DC Motor

A conductor carrying current placed in a magnetic field experiences a force: F = BIL sinθ.

  • Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Used to find force direction. Forefinger = Magnetic Field, Middle finger = Current, Thumb = Force/Motion[cite: 1].
  • DC Motor: Converts electrical energy into mechanical energy. Uses a split-ring commutator to reverse current direction every half cycle, ensuring continuous rotation[cite: 1].

3. Electromagnetic Induction (EMI)

The phenomenon of producing an induced EMF (and current) in a circuit due to a changing magnetic flux[cite: 1].

  • Faraday's Law: The magnitude of induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage[cite: 1].
  • Lenz's Law: The direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the very cause that produced it. It is a consequence of the Law of Conservation of Energy[cite: 1].
  • AC Generator: Converts mechanical energy to electrical energy using EMI. Uses slip rings for continuous alternating output[cite: 1].

4. Mutual Induction & Transformers

Mutual induction is the production of an induced EMF in a secondary coil due to changing current in a nearby primary coil.

  • Transformer: Operates on mutual induction to step up or step down AC voltages[cite: 1].
  • Equation: Vs / Vp = Ns / Np = Ip / Is[cite: 1].
  • Step-Up: Ns > Np (Increases voltage, decreases current)[cite: 1].
  • Step-Down: Ns < Np (Decreases voltage, increases current)[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 16: BASIC ELECTRONICS

1. Thermionic Emission & Cathode Rays

  • Thermionic Emission: The process of emitting electrons from the surface of a hot metal (e.g., a tungsten filament heated by an electric current).
  • Cathode Rays: Beams of fast-moving electrons. They travel in straight lines, cast shadows, can be deflected by electric and magnetic fields, and produce fluorescence.
  • Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO): An instrument used to display rapidly changing electrical signals. Components include: Electron Gun (produces electrons), Deflecting Plates (sweep the beam horizontally/vertically), and a Fluorescent Screen[cite: 1].

2. Analogue and Digital Electronics

  • Analogue Quantities: Vary continuously with time (e.g., temperature, time, pressure, analogue voltage). Susceptible to signal degradation[cite: 1].
  • Digital Quantities: Have discrete values, typically represented as binary states (0 and 1, Low and High, Off and On). Digital circuits are robust against noise and form the basis of computers[cite: 1].
  • ADC and DAC: Analogue-to-Digital Converters and Digital-to-Analogue Converters act as bridges between the physical (analogue) world and digital processors.

3. Logic Gates & Boolean Algebra

Logic gates are fundamental digital circuits that perform basic logical operations based on Boolean algebra.

  • AND Gate: Output is 1 (True) ONLY if all inputs are 1. Boolean equation: Y = A · B[cite: 1].
  • OR Gate: Output is 1 if AT LEAST ONE input is 1. Boolean equation: Y = A + B[cite: 1].
  • NOT Gate (Inverter): Has one input. It inverts the input (1 becomes 0, 0 becomes 1). Boolean equation: Y = Ā[cite: 1].
  • NAND Gate: An AND gate followed by a NOT gate. Output is 0 only when both inputs are 1. Known as a universal gate[cite: 1]. Y = (A · B)̄.
  • NOR Gate: An OR gate followed by a NOT gate. Output is 1 only when both inputs are 0. Universal gate[cite: 1]. Y = (A + B)̄.

CHAPTER 17: INFORMATION & COMMUNICATION TECHNOLOGY

1. Introduction to ICT

ICT is the scientific method used to store, arrange, transmit, and receive data globally[cite: 1]. Data refers to raw facts, while Information is processed, organized, and meaningful data[cite: 1].

2. Computer Based Information System (CBIS)

A CBIS has five critical components[cite: 1]:

  • Hardware: Physical parts of the computer (CPU, monitor, keyboard).
  • Software: Programs that direct the hardware (OS, applications).
  • Data: The raw figures entered into the system.
  • Procedures: The set of rules and guidelines for using the system.
  • People: Users and administrators of the system.

3. Transmission of Information

  • Electrical Signals: Transmitted via copper wires (e.g., landline telephones). Prone to resistance and energy loss.
  • Radio/Microwaves: Wireless transmission via space/atmosphere (e.g., cell networks, satellites). Used for long-distance communication[cite: 1].
  • Optical Fibers: Use Total Internal Reflection (TIR) to transmit data as pulses of light[cite: 1]. Advantages include massive bandwidth, no electromagnetic interference, and low signal loss over long distances[cite: 1].

4. Storage Devices

  • Primary Memory: RAM (Volatile, temporary storage for active tasks) and ROM (Non-volatile, stores boot firmware)[cite: 1].
  • Secondary Storage: Hard Disk Drives (magnetic), SSDs (flash memory, faster/no moving parts), and Optical Disks (CD/DVD utilizing lasers)[cite: 1].

5. Internet and Cyber Ethics

The Internet is a global network of computers communicating via TCP/IP protocols[cite: 1]. Services include the WWW (World Wide Web), e-mail, and e-commerce[cite: 1].

Risks & Ethics: Includes hacking, malware (viruses, worms), piracy, and phishing. Cybersecurity measures include strong encryption, anti-virus software, firewalls, and managing one's digital footprint[cite: 1].

CHAPTER 18: ATOMIC & NUCLEAR PHYSICS

1. Atomic Structure & Isotopes

The atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) orbited by negative electrons[cite: 1].

  • Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus[cite: 1].
  • Mass Number (A): The total number of protons + neutrons (nucleons)[cite: 1].
  • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same Atomic Number (Z) but different Mass Numbers (A) due to different numbers of neutrons (e.g., Hydrogen has Protium ¹H, Deuterium ²H, Tritium ³H)[cite: 1].

2. Natural Radioactivity

The spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei (typically heavy elements with Z > 82)[cite: 1].

  • Alpha (α) Particles: Helium nuclei (⁴He²⁺). Charge: +2. Highly ionizing, but low penetrating power (stopped by a sheet of paper)[cite: 1].
  • Beta (β) Particles: Fast-moving electrons (e⁻). Charge: -1. Moderate ionizing and penetrating power (stopped by aluminum foil)[cite: 1].
  • Gamma (γ) Rays: High-energy electromagnetic photons. Charge: 0. Low ionizing ability but extremely high penetrating power (requires thick lead or concrete to stop)[cite: 1].

Background Radiation: Low-level radiation naturally present in the environment (from cosmic rays, rocks, soil).

3. Half-Life & Nuclear Transmutation

  • Half-Life (T½): The time required for half of the unstable radioactive atoms in a sample to decay[cite: 1].
  • Decay Formula: N = N₀ × (1/2)ⁿ, where 'n' is the number of half-lives elapsed (n = t / T½)[cite: 1].
  • Transmutation: Changing one element into another via radioactive decay. (e.g., Alpha decay decreases A by 4 and Z by 2).

4. Fission and Fusion

  • Nuclear Fission: Splitting a heavy nucleus (like U-235) into two lighter nuclei upon absorbing a slow neutron. Releases massive energy and 2-3 more neutrons, which can trigger a chain reaction. Controlled in nuclear reactors, uncontrolled in atomic bombs[cite: 1].
  • Nuclear Fusion: Combining two light nuclei (like Deuterium and Tritium) to form a heavier nucleus (Helium), releasing huge amounts of energy. Powers the stars/Sun. Very difficult to achieve on Earth due to extreme temperature and pressure requirements[cite: 1].

5. Uses of Radioisotopes & Safety

Uses: Carbon-14 for radiometric dating of fossils. Cobalt-60 for cancer radiotherapy. Iodine-131 for thyroid imaging. Tracers in agriculture and pipelines[cite: 1].

Hazards: Radiation burns, genetic mutations, cancer. Safety: Use lead shielding, limit exposure time (ALARA principle), wear dosimeters, and handle with remote tongs[cite: 1].
